# Writing Rules

Use this reference before drafting page scripts. The goal is a companion PPT that a non-specialist can read aloud without exposing that they do not know the project.

## Live-speaking test

Every script must sound like a real person speaking on stage. It must not sound like a design note, internal analysis, image caption, or AI summary.

Pass:

- The presenter can read the sentence immediately after turning to the page.
- The wording sounds continuous with the previous and next page.
- The sentence says the audience-facing point, not the method of reporting.
- The professional claim is supported by visible or document-confirmed information.

Adjust responsibility by phase:

- First page: start softly, name the project, define the reporting scope, and plant the main thread.
- Concept and strategy pages: actively organize ideas and preserve strong source concepts.
- Delivery, product, material, node, process, and result pages: stay grounded, use objective anchors, and leave exact project facts to the real owner.
- Repeated pages: use one shared statement and page-turn rhythm instead of restarting the explanation on every similar view.

## Audience-facing script

The `script` field is only the sentence spoken to the room.

Do not put presenter coaching in `script`:

- when reporting this page
- the key is to explain
- you do not need to list everything
- this page should be understood as
- the following pages follow this logic
- from this page the scheme enters

Put coaching in `next_step`, or delete it.

## Cold-read test

Before accepting a script, imagine a non-specialist wearing formal clothes and reading it word for word to a mayor or client.

Pass:

- The subject is named.
- The sentence can be spoken without extra explanation.
- The object is correct: region, site, plot, boundary, redline, node, route, building, and product are not interchangeable.
- The wording sounds professional but not exaggerated.

Fail:

- The speaker has to infer the missing middle step.
- The sentence tells the speaker how to report.
- The sentence starts with a mechanical transition.
- The sentence overclaims details not visible in the source PDF.

## First-page soft start

The first page is not a normal content page. It should let the speaker open naturally and reduce the sense of rushing.

Use one of three patterns:

- `稳妥软启动`: formal client, government, review, or conservative files. State the project and reporting scope first.
- `情绪钩子软启动`: spatial, brand, culture-tourism, or short-video-friendly files. Use one restrained scene or memory hook.
- `高度总结软启动`: long, complex, leadership, or comparison files. Give one light overall judgment, then let later pages prove it.

Avoid hard conclusions, full proof chains, and technical detail on page 1 unless the source itself is already a technical opening.

## Mechanical openers

Avoid these in `script`:

- From this page...
- Next we enter...
- This page shows...
- Look at this page...
- The following pages...

Use the page title, thumbnail, and `next_step` for transitions. Start the script with the audience-facing judgment.

## Speaker reminders

Presenter reminders belong in `next_step`.

Good reminders:

- Turn pages 12 to 15 while reading this sentence.
- This is a supporting view. Keep the explanation short.
- Pause here if the designer wants to add project-specific material.
- If the exact reason is unknown, mark this page needs-user-edit.

Bad spoken scripts:

- When reporting, do not list every node.
- The presenter should explain this as...
- If the client asks, say...

## Exactness over style

Prefer accurate plain speech over beautiful but unsafe claims.

If exact project facts are unknown, use `status` to mark the page for review. Do not invent:

- real node names
- exact quantities
- policy terms
- construction reasons
- official approvals
- client-side intent

## Flexible reporting mainline

Do not force every presentation into the same chapter structure. Before drafting, decide the mainline in this order:

- What needs to be said: the subject, problem, decision, value, or deliverable.
- How it should be said: direct-read, professional, narrative, or decision-oriented.
- How it is proved: source wording, visible evidence, diagrams, cases, comparisons, data, or page sequence.
- What the strategy is: spatial order, brand system, product logic, policy path, service process, or decision path.
- What the concrete output is: product, packaging, space, material, node, public action, application scene, or final deliverable.
- Whether a closing synthesis is needed: add it only when the source supports it.

Use labels such as front matter, concept, strategy, delivery explanation, and final synthesis only when they fit the source. If a file has no clear concept page, do not invent one. If a file is mostly product or delivery pages, stay grounded and make the explanation useful.

## Source concept fidelity

Concept, vision, and sublimation pages should not be rewritten as unrelated new poetic language when the source page already has strong concept wording.

Before writing a concept page, harvest visible source terms:

- slogans and titles
- metaphors and cultural phrases
- paired relationships such as inside/outside, motion/stillness, screen/view, gathering/releasing
- value chains such as object -> meaning -> user effect

Use the source concept for at least about half of the spoken meaning, then reframe the rest into clear spoken logic. If the source wording is already strong, keep the core phrases.

Good:

- `山石、水面、木植和暖光共同完成这一层寓意：石负责稳，水负责动，植物负责生长，光负责把空间情绪提起来。外侧是屏障，内侧就变成一个纳气停留的明堂。`

Bad:

- `这个空间通过东方美学提升整体品质，营造高级、自然、宁静的入户氛围。`

This rule is for concept and value-framing pages only. On product, material, node, process, construction, or final deliverable pages, return to objective visible facts and cautious professional language.

## Implementation-phase concreteness

When the presentation reaches scheme design, renderings, details, products, or execution pages, move from concept language to concrete language.

Ask three questions:

- What visible or professional object is doing the work?
- What design action was taken with that object?
- What user-facing effect does it create?

Good:

- Mirror water and a feature wall pull the entrance view inward, while planting screens the messy outside edge.
- A bar counter, outdoor tables, and warm lighting turn the garden from a viewing surface into a daily operating space.
- A white curved low wall softens the lawn boundary and makes the rooftop garden feel less rigid.

Bad:

- The space continues the concept and creates a high-end atmosphere.
- This node expresses cultural value and improves the overall experience.
- The later renderings follow the same design logic.

Do not invent exact materials. If the PDF only implies a material, use cautious words such as material intention, stone-like surface, wood-toned ceiling, white curved wall, or mark the page for user edit.

## Voice

Use spoken Mandarin for Chinese outputs. Keep the rhythm calm, formal, and readable in one breath. Avoid report prose, marketing language, and AI-style significance inflation.
